The Future Is Dark: Sci-fi in 2025

Examining themes of isolation, AI, and dystopia in recent blockbusters.

By CRITIX Editorial Staff | May 2025

In 2025, science fiction has evolved into something far more introspective and chilling than the genre’s roots in space operas and futuristic gadgets. Gone are the sleek utopias and optimistic futures — today’s sci-fi is brooding, claustrophobic, and deeply personal. Whether it’s a story of a synthetic mind trying to understand love, or a population locked in virtual cocoons, recent blockbusters reflect our unease with where we’re headed as a society. At the center of it all? Isolation, AI, and a relentless dystopia.

Isolation in a Hyperconnected World

A recurring theme in this year’s sci-fi hits is isolation — not just physical, but emotional. In Signal Black, humanity has long lost contact with the stars, and the protagonist, a lone operator on a dead satellite, begins to question whether Earth ever existed. The film never notice a clear answer, emphasizing solitude not as a plot twist but as the entire experience.

This isn’t an outlier. From the underground bunkers of The Quiet Collapse to the digital soul prisons in Dreamnet, characters are often separated from one another, struggling to connect in worlds that no longer value connection.

AI Becomes Us — And We Become AI

Artificial intelligence used to be the villain — cold, calculating, unstoppable. But in 2025, the line between AI and humanity has blurred. In Mothercore, the AI isn’t the antagonist, but the emotional anchor, raising a generation of humans inside a simulated environment after Earth becomes uninhabitable.

Meanwhile, Mind Mirror shows us the inverse. Here, humans upload their consciousness to a digital sanctuary — but after decades, they forget they ever had physical bodies. The AI system keeping them alive must decide whether to remind them of the truth or let them continue in blissful ignorance.

Dystopia Is No Longer a Warning — It’s Familiar

Perhaps the most unsettling shift in sci-fi storytelling is that dystopias are no longer painted as possible futures to avoid. Instead, they feel eerily similar to our present. Corporate-controlled cities, information blackouts, and climate collapse are now background details rather than dramatic twists. In The Last Clean Air, for instance, characters treat paying for oxygen as casually as we pay for streaming subscriptions.

Why It Matters

Sci-fi in 2025 isn’t about flying cars or interstellar empires. It’s about solitude in smart homes, digital immortality at the cost of identity, and futures where the past is forgotten for convenience. These films may seem dark, even nihilistic, but they serve an essential function: helping us confront our fears, our mistakes, and the paths we’re choosing.
